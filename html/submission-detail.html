The Glass Garden

The garden had always been there, or so Elena believed. Tucked behind the crumbling stone wall of her grandmother's estate, it existed in a state of perpetual twilight, as if the sun itself had forgotten this particular corner of the world.

She discovered it on a Tuesday in late October, when the maples had surrendered their last leaves and the air carried the sharp promise of frost. The gate was iron, rusted into a permanent half-open position, and beyond it stretched rows of impossible flowers — roses, lilies, orchids — all rendered in glass so fine it caught the fading light and scattered it into constellations across the frozen earth.

"Grandmother never mentioned this," Elena whispered, reaching out to touch a translucent petal. It was warm beneath her fingertips, humming with something that might have been life. The glass was not cold and dead as she had expected. It pulsed with color: deep crimsons that shifted to violet, golds that rippled into copper, greens so vivid they put the memory of summer to shame.

She walked deeper into the garden, her boots crunching on gravel paths that wound between beds of crystalline blooms. A fountain stood at the center, its basin filled not with water but with liquid light — mercury-bright and slow-moving, reflecting a sky that didn't match the one above...
